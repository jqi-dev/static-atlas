Quantum Entanglement

No, it doesn't allow faster-than-light communication.

Entanglement twists together two or more quantum particles, replacing their individual identities with an entwined whole.

Of all the peculiar things that occur in the quantum world, none is more likely to sprain the brain than entanglement. Einstein thought it was totally whacko, but over the past 50 years physicists have accumulated a huge body of experimental evidence that entanglement is real. Weird, but real.

It works like this: As a result of some quantum processes,⊕ ⊕ For example, a particle with 0 spin might decay into two entangled particles with -1/2 and +1/2 spin. Alternatively, when a laser beam travels through a certain kind of “nonlinear” crystal, it sometimes splits into pairs of entangled photons.two or more objects (photons, electrons, atoms, etc.) become so inextricably intertwined—"entangled"—that even if they are physically separated by a great distance, they have a shared connection and cannot be described independently.

Entanglement persists even when entangled particles are seperated.

This leads to seemingly incredible outcomes: When you measure a property of one of a pair of entangled quantum objects (such as its momentum, polarization, or spin), and then measure the same property on the other object, the results are always, invariably correlated. It looks suspiciously like the measurement of the first object instantaneously determines the properties of the second object.

Whoa. That kind of behavior seems to be completely at odds with a bedrock quantum principle—namely, that a particle does not have specific properties until it is measured. Before being measured it exists in a superposition of properties with different odds of occurring.

That’s also true for entangled particles. Measuring a property of one of them will still result in a random value that was impossible to know prior to the measurement. But the corresponding property of the other particle will always be exactly correlated. That’s because entangled particles are not separate, individual entities. They’re both parts of a single quantum whole.

Imagine two entangled photons that fly apart until they are on opposite sides of the galaxy. Each has a chance of being “spin up” or “spin down,” and their entanglement means that if someone measures the spin of Photon 1 and finds that it is up, then the spin of Photon 2 is down. Or vice versa. Every time. This happens despite the fact that there is no possible way for information about the result of Photon 1’s measurement to reach Photon 2—even if it traveled at the speed of light.

Einstein balked at that, dismissing entanglement as “spooky action at a distance.” He believed that there must be some so-far-unknown “hidden variables” that exist prior to the measurement that could explain the inevitable correlations.

In the 1960s, a test was devised to rule out the existence of hidden variables. It was ferociously difficult to perform, but after decades of increasingly sophisticated work, many teams of physicists have all but the shut the door on the hidden variables idea. One of the most accurate tests to date occurred in 2015, and it dismissed many lingering alternative interpretations, known as “loopholes.” That did not “prove” the existence of entanglement; “proof” is hard to come by in science. But it did leave less room to explain entanglement by other means.

One more strange feature of entanglement is that two entangled particles (photons, say) that interact with two other kinds of particles (such as atoms) can entangle the atoms by proxy. That phenomenon is now being exploited in quantum information processing to transfer quantum states among various components of a system. It’s all but assured that any future quantum computer will harness the power of entanglement.
